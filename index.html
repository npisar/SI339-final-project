"Tell me about that drum thing you do"

It's really pretty simple.

I've loved playing the snare drum since 5th grade, and specifically the marching snare drum since 7th grade. I've taken that passion to an extreme most people don't know exists.

During the winter and summer, I rehearse and perform with Winter Guard International (WGI) and Drum Corps International (DCI) ensembles, respectively (pages about DCI coming soon!). In short, those are governing bodies that organize professional marching bands or indoor drumline ensembles scattered around the country.

This website only covers how I got involved with WGI and the details about what the activity is (for now). Keep reading to learn about how I started drumming in the first place and how I found WGI. Or, click one of the links in the header to learn about how WGI works or my experience with it.

How I Started Marching Drumming

Before I experienced anything musical through my schools, my parents enrolled me in piano lessons. That was really key for me-it got me involved with and excited about music super early on in my life. I took those lessons and got other general music lessons in elementary school until I eventually became old enough to choose an instrument for 5th grade band class. I started with a bell kit and small drumpad.

As I continued in band class throughout middle school, I remember seeing a drum cam video by an alumni of my high school. He was playing through the school cadence series marching into the stadium, and I thought it was the coolest thing in the world.

"I want to do that," I remember thinking to myself. I started taking lessons with an instructor local to my area around the beginning of my eighth grade year. I worked extremely hard with him, probably harder than I had ever worked at something in my life. I wanted to make snare drum in the Dow High Drumline, and there were a lot of veteran members. I don't know what made me so interested in snare drum specifically; maybe it was the strange "traditional grip" used in the left hand, maybe it was that they were a always present high voice in the drumline sound. Regardless, after months of lessons and tons of hard work, it was time for the audition. And I got it! I made the snare line. I was also the only freshman to do so. You could say I felt pretty good about it, and I was definitely a little too cocky about it, especially considering what I know now about marching snare drumming!

So that's how I started playing the marching snare drum. But how did I end up going from Dow High Marching Band to spending thousands of dollars and nearly all of my free time to wear goofy outfits, run around a gym or football field, and practice the snare drum every day?

Why I Do It

Why do I do this? Why do I care about drums so much? Why do I spend so much time working on this when it takes up my weekends?

I was always motivated to be a better musician. It's simply an inate drive I have. It's something I'm passionate about and love--it's as simple as that. I like long hours practicing. I like everything about it. I really started the grind of "getting good" during high school when my friend Bruce (who I was on the Dow High Drumline with) showed me a bunch of stuff about drum corps, the Michigan State Drumline (which he auditioned for and made), and when he was generally better than me. I wanted to drum better like he did.

Like I said, I discovered indoor and drum corps through those early high school experiences. I kept watching, and I thought it was the coolest thing in the world. I wanted to do it.

Why do I still do it, though? Well, I still love it. I love every part of it. I love driving to rehearsal with my friends. I love spending the night and complaining after a really tough day. I truly feel at home at rehearsals, performances, and anything related to either activity. I do it because of the people, the community. Everyone in the drumline world is so close to each other and, as a rule, wants each other to succeed. It's generally an extremely positive community focused on growth, education, and both self-improvement and group-improvement. I love the people who I've marched with in a special way. Regardless of whether we're best friends or not, I still have some form of love for everyone I've marched with.

I still do it because I've learned a ton of valuable life lessons. I'm a better communicator. I react to tough situations better than I used to. I'm more willing or ready for things to change quickly and have to adjust. I'm a better leader. I'm a better listener. I know what it means to work on something for a long time, and for it to succeed. I know what it means to work on something for a long, and for it to not work out. There's a lot of things I didn't realize I was picking up on that have made their way into my daily life.

And finally, I still do it because there are only a handful of people in the world who are trully excellent at something. That's something special. Not many people spend the 10,000 hours (or whatever arbitrary number you want to assign that it takes to master something). If I have the chance to be the best in the world at something, it would be insanely silly to pass up that chance or not put in the work.
